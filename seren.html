Seren

In Welsh, the language of Rachel's home country, Seren means star. This word has been at the centre of the work since long before the work had a name.

Welsh star. The word itself: light in the dark, noticing, the thing you steer by.
A daughter's middle name. Ruby Seren — whose small story, The Loveheart, became the constitution of everything that followed.
The pen name in the dark. serenflower — the name Rachel wrote under from the side of a hospital bed, in the hardest year. Those posts were the first observational work on what would become Voice Quotient™, though no one knew it then.
The company. Serenwood, founded 2009 — the same legal entity, sixteen years later, now called PowerVox. The 2025 rebrand was not a pivot; it was the company catching up with the word that had been at its heart the whole time.
The receptive dimension. In Enfys, Seren is the character who gives Signal its face. She stands with Ada (IQ), Ceri (EQ) and Will (VQ) in the story, but Signal is not a fourth intelligence beside them; it is the receptive dimension within Voice Quotient™. Some words travel ahead of their meaning.

"Although Seren means star in Welsh, it's also within the word serendipity, and this is a word I come back to often too." Serendipity: the occurrence of making fortunate discoveries by accident — finding value, meaning or beauty that you were not actively looking for. It implies chance, curiosity, meaning-making and perception together, and carries a quiet idea of prepared openness — the ability to recognise value in the unforeseen. Think of penicillin, discovered when mould contaminated a lab dish. There have been many small moments along the journey where, looking back, the dots connect — and they continue to show up, like stars guiding the way. Now they are noticed, they are easier to follow.

As Louis Pasteur said: "Chance favours the prepared mind."
