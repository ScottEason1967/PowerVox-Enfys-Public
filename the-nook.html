The Nook

Off the Library sits a quiet nook where the works the model leans on are gathered and kept open. This is the evidence register: the third-party research the village cites, and its own papers and charters, set side by side so anyone can see exactly what is backed and what is not yet.

What this nook is for.

The Observatory holds the research standard — the beta, the claims ladder, the thresholds a claim must clear before it may be made in public. This nook holds the research itself: a register of the actual sources. Two kinds sit here, kept honestly apart. External research is third-party work the model draws on, cited with its author, title and year, and linked where a source gives a link. Our own research is PowerVox's position papers, charters and companions, named plainly as internal work product with its version and date, never dressed up as independent evidence. Where the model makes a claim that nothing yet backs, a slot is left open and marked, so the gaps are visible rather than hidden.

How to read the markers. Each external item carries a confidence tag from the source dossier itself. Rigorous is peer-reviewed, large-sample, or longitudinal work. Widely cited, weakly sourced is a figure used by serious institutions whose ultimate provenance is harder to trace, and which is attributed carefully rather than presented as primary research. Internal works carry PowerVox. The category claim the whole model points at — that Voice Intelligence sits alongside IQ and EQ — is held at hypothesis until the beta clears its thresholds. Nothing here asserts it.
External research Third-party studies

Independent work the model cites, drawn from the Greenhouse research companion Money in the Hands of Women, which assembled and graded these sources. Each is reproduced here with the author, title, year and link the companion records. The companion's own confidence grading is carried across unchanged.

The largest wealth transfer in history is underway, and it is moving toward women.
US women controlled roughly $18tn in investable assets in 2023, projected to rise to $34tn (about 38% of US investable assets) by 2030; female-controlled wealth grew 51% from 2018 to 2023 against a 43% market rise. Based on a survey of 13,000+ US and European investors plus McKinsey wealth-management data — the strongest single source in the dossier.
SourceMcKinsey & Company — The new face of wealth: the rise of the female investorMay 2025Rigorous
Income reaching the mother specifically improves children's outcomes.
A systematic review of RCTs, quasi-experiments and longitudinal studies found household income has a positive causal effect on children's cognitive, social-behavioural and health outcomes, with larger effects where the income increase came through the mother.
SourceCooper & Stewart — Does household income affect children's outcomes?, Child Indicators Research2020Rigorous
A modest income rise to the poorest households produces measurable downstream gains.
A US natural experiment found an additional $4,000 per year to the poorest households produced one additional year of educational attainment by age 21 and a 22% reduction in minor crime at ages 16–17.
SourceAkee, Copeland, Keeler, Angold & Costello — Parents' Incomes and Children's Outcomes (NBER)NBERRigorous
Women invest with longer horizons and toward sustainable and impact-aligned ends.
McKinsey's investor survey found women more security- and long-horizon-oriented in their goals; Morgan Stanley found 84% of women express interest in sustainable investing vs 67% of men, with women more likely to direct capital toward equity and environmental causes. The precise sustainable-investing split is attributed to Morgan Stanley directly.
SourceMcKinsey (May 2025); Morgan Stanley Institute for Sustainable Investing (via Stanford Social Innovation Review)2025Rigorous
Firms led by female CEOs show measurably stronger ESG performance.
A peer-reviewed study found female-led firms demonstrate stronger ESG performance and that female-led ESG initiatives positively affect both accounting and market performance — read by the authors as evidence the activity is substantive rather than performative.
SourceDo female CEOs lead more ethical firms? International Review of Financial Analysis2025Rigorous
Women give more, and the disposition behind it is structural, not circumstantial.
Twenty years of nationally representative longitudinal research from the Women's Philanthropy Institute establishes consistent gender differences in giving; peer-reviewed work finds women's higher giving is mediated by higher empathic concern and a "principle of care" orientation.
SourceWomen's Philanthropy Institute, Indiana University; De Wit & Bekkers — Nonprofit and Voluntary Sector Quarterly2016 / 20-yr seriesRigorous
Female-founded teams outperform on returns per dollar invested.
BCG/MassChallenge found women-owned startups generate more revenue per dollar of investment; First Round Capital, reviewing ten years and 300 portfolio companies, found teams with a female co-founder performed 63% better by valuation than all-male teams. The current capital system under-allocates relative to performance.
SourceBCG / MassChallenge — Why Women-Owned Startups Are a Better Bet (2018); First Round Capital — 10 Year Project2018Rigorous
The market mistakes overconfidence for competence, and pays for the mistake.
Analysing 35,000+ brokerage accounts, Barber & Odean found men trade 45% more than women and lose roughly 0.94 percentage points a year more to over-trading; among single investors the gap widens. The mechanism is overconfidence — a measurable, costly bias the system rewards rather than corrects. This is the load-bearing external anchor for the "signal over volume" argument.
SourceBarber & Odean — Boys Will Be Boys, Quarterly Journal of Economics2001Rigorous
The advisory industry is structurally mismatched for the inheriting female client.
Cerulli projects $124tn transferring through 2048, the largest in record. McKinsey finds 53% of female-controlled assets unmanaged (vs 45% male); women prefer educator-advisors and report a confidence gap before inheritance that closes sharply after it. Around 80% of widows switch advisors within a year.
SourceCerulli Associates — The Great Wealth Transfer (Dec 2024); McKinsey (May 2025); Citizens Wealth — Women & Wealth Transfer Survey (Mar 2025)2024–25Rigorous
Women reinvest the large majority of their earnings into family and community.
The figure of up to 90% reinvestment by women versus 30–40% by men is cited by the World Bank, IMF, Harvard Center for International Development, the Gates Foundation and UN Women. Traced back, it leads to advocacy publications of 2003–2009 rather than a single foundational peer-reviewed study. The companion's own instruction: attribute as a "widely cited" figure, not as primary research. The directional claim is supported by adjacent rigorous work on female-headed household spending; the precise percentages are not.
SourceWidely cited by the World Bank, IMF, Harvard CID and Gates Foundation2003–2009 originWidely cited, weakly sourced
Women will control the majority of global capital by the 2030s.
A stronger framing that secondary reporting has occasionally extrapolated from McKinsey's work. McKinsey's primary source does not make it; what it shows is that female-controlled wealth is growing faster than male-controlled wealth and that in the US and EU women will control 38–47% of investable assets by 2030. The companion's instruction: frame as "approaching parity over the 2030s and beyond," not definitive parity by a date.
SourceSecondary extrapolation of McKinsey (May 2025) — not asserted in the primary source—Widely cited, weakly sourced

From our own research PowerVox work product

The village's own papers, charters and companions. These are internal work product, not independent evidence. They set positions, frame the research, and assemble external sources — and they are cited honestly as such. The category claim they build toward is held at hypothesis until the beta clears its thresholds.

Voice Intelligence is the leadership faculty; Voice Quotient™ is its measure.
The naming position the category rests on, modelled on intelligence and IQ. States plainly that the naming may be adopted now but the public claim it implies — that Voice Intelligence sits alongside IQ and EQ — is held at hypothesis until the beta validates. Carries its own triggers for reopening. Adopted internally; claim status: held at hypothesis.
SourcePowerVox Position Paper PP-01 · Voice Intelligence & Voice Quotient™v0.1 · May 2026PowerVox
The compensation hypothesis: lost receptive VQ drives compensation, burnout risk and eroded safety.
The ten-year research framing. Sets out the mechanistic, falsifiable hypothesis — that when leaders lose Signal, Authenticity and Presence they compensate through over-control, masking, cognitive overload and relational disconnection, raising burnout risk and lowering psychological safety. A hypothesis under investigation, not a finding. Names its own blindspots, including reverse causality and untested buyer demand.
SourcePowerVox · 10 Year Framing for Research (internal strategy paper)2026PowerVox

Template stage; the substantive content is to be drafted across the beta.
